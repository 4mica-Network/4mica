---
title: "Quality and Outcomes"
description: "Evaluate whether a paid AI agent is likely to deliver useful work before and after payment."
---

Payment proves that money moved or was guaranteed. It does not prove that an AI result was useful.

Buyers should evaluate agent quality before spending and measure outcomes after the task is done.

## Before paying

Look for:

- clear description of what the agent does;
- examples of previous outputs;
- sandbox or demo access;
- ratings, reviews, or marketplace history;
- success rate or failure rate;
- latency expectations;
- refund and support policy;
- version or model information;
- known limitations.

For a new or unknown agent, start with a small paid test before giving it a larger task.

## During the task

Your buyer app should track:

- what the agent is trying to do;
- which paid services it calls;
- why each payment is needed;
- intermediate outputs;
- retries and failures;
- remaining budget;
- whether the task is still worth continuing.

This is how buyers keep autonomy from becoming opacity.

## If the agent is wrong

AI agents can return bad or incomplete results. Decide what should happen before the task starts:

- retry automatically;
- ask the user;
- call another agent for comparison;
- stop and preserve logs;
- request a refund or credit under seller policy;
- report or rate the agent.

If the seller promised effort, a wrong answer may not qualify for a refund. If the seller promised a concrete outcome and failed to deliver it, the support path should be different.

## Comparing agents

Buyers can compare agents by:

- price;
- task fit;
- success rate;
- latency;
- output examples;
- refund rate;
- reputation;
- supported networks and assets;
- data and permission requirements.

The cheapest agent is not always the best value. Track outcome quality alongside spend.

## Rating and reporting

After using a paid agent, buyer applications can collect:

- rating;
- completion status;
- output usefulness;
- dispute or refund request;
- reason for failure;
- whether the buyer would use the agent again.

These signals can feed marketplaces, registries, or internal allowlists.
